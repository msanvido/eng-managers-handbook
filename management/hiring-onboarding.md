## Hiring

Building a great team starts with hiring the right people. A thoughtful, structured hiring process is essential for growing your engineering organization.

### Hiring Planning

* **Quarterly hiring plan**: Align hiring with business objectives and team capacity
* **Headcount allocation**: Distribute headcount across teams based on priorities and workload
* **Timeline considerations**: Account for lengthy interview processes (typically 4-8 weeks from req to offer)
* **Budget planning**: Plan for compensation, relocation, equipment, and onboarding costs

### Job Descriptions and Interview Plans

**Job Descriptions:**
* Be specific about the role, responsibilities, and required skills
* Include technology stack and tools the team uses
* Set clear expectations about the level (junior, mid, senior, staff, etc.)
* Describe team culture and working environment
* Highlight growth opportunities and learning paths

**Interview Plans:**
* Design a consistent interview process for each role level
* Include multiple interview stages:
  * Initial phone screen (30 min)
  * Technical screen (1 hour coding or system design)
  * Onsite/virtual panel (4-5 hours with multiple interviewers)
  * Final culture fit and leadership discussion
* Assign interviewers with diverse perspectives
* Define evaluation criteria and scorecards for each interview

### Hiring Manager Role and Interviewer Panel

**Hiring Manager Responsibilities:**
* Own the req from opening to close
* Define role requirements and success criteria
* Review all candidates and make final hiring decisions
* Coordinate with recruiting and schedule interviews
* Ensure consistent evaluation across candidates
* Make offers and close candidates

**Interviewer Panel:**
* Assemble diverse interviewers (different levels, backgrounds, teams)
* Provide interview training and calibration
* Assign specific areas to evaluate (coding, system design, culture fit, leadership, etc.)
* Conduct debrief sessions after each candidate
* Provide timely, structured feedback

### References

* Always check references before making an offer
* Ask specific questions about performance, collaboration, and growth areas
* Back-channel references (when appropriate and with candidate permission)
* Validate claims about past work and achievements

## Giving Offers

Making an offer is a critical moment in the hiring process. Handle it thoughtfully to maximize acceptance rates.

### Total Compensation Discussion

**Components to discuss:**
* **Base salary**: Research market rates for the role and location
* **Equity compensation**: Options or RSUs (see below)
* **Bonuses**: Performance bonuses, signing bonuses, relocation assistance
* **Benefits**: Health insurance, 401k matching, PTO, etc.
* **Vesting schedule**: Typically 4 years with 1-year cliff
* **Refresh grants**: How equity is refreshed for top performers

**Best Practices:**
* Be transparent about compensation philosophy
* Explain the full compensation package, not just salary
* Provide context on equity value and potential
* Be prepared to negotiate within defined bands
* Move quickly—top candidates have multiple offers

### Job Description and Responsibilities

In the offer letter, clearly state:
* Job title and reporting structure
* Key responsibilities and initial projects
* Start date and location/remote policy
* Probation period (if applicable)
* At-will employment terms

## Understanding Options and RSUs

Equity compensation can be confusing for candidates. As a hiring manager, you should be able to explain:

### Stock Options

* **What they are**: The right to purchase company stock at a fixed price (strike price)
* **Vesting**: Typically 4 years with a 1-year cliff
* **Exercise window**: Period after leaving to exercise options (typically 90 days, some companies offer 10 years)
* **Taxation**: ISOs vs NSOs, tax implications on exercise and sale
* **409A valuation**: How strike price is determined

### RSUs (Restricted Stock Units)

* **What they are**: Company stock granted to you that vests over time
* **Vesting**: Similar schedule to options (4 years, 1-year cliff)
* **Taxation**: Taxed as income when they vest
* **Liquidity**: More valuable at public companies, less certain at private companies

### Explaining Equity Value

* Explain the current valuation and share price
* Discuss potential upside (and risks)
* Compare equity value to cash compensation
* Be honest about liquidity timelines (IPO, acquisition, secondary market)

## Onboarding

First impressions matter. A structured onboarding process sets new hires up for success.

### 30-60-90 Day Plans

Create a standard onboarding plan template and customize for each hire:

**First 30 Days (Learning):**
* Complete onboarding checklist (access, tools, documentation)
* Meet with team members and key stakeholders
* Shadow experienced engineers
* Make small contributions (bug fixes, documentation)
* Understand codebase architecture and development workflow
* Learn team processes and rituals

**Days 31-60 (Contributing):**
* Take on first substantive project
* Participate in code reviews
* Begin regular 1:1s with manager
* Start building relationships across teams
* Contribute to team discussions and planning

**Days 61-90 (Ownership):**
* Own a feature or component end-to-end
* Lead a small project or initiative
* Mentor or help onboard newer team members
* Demonstrate proficiency in team's tech stack
* Receive first performance feedback

### Onboarding Best Practices

* Assign an onboarding buddy (different from manager)
* Schedule regular check-ins (daily first week, then weekly)
* Provide clear short-term goals and success criteria
* Over-communicate expectations and context
* Solicit feedback on the onboarding process

## Attrition

People leave companies. How you handle attrition defines your organization's culture and reputation.

### Types of Attrition

**Regrettable Attrition:**

This is the worst—losing high performers you wanted to retain. Management's goal is to minimize this.

* **Counteroffers**: Can we match the offer?
  * Often this is a temporary solution since money is often not the root cause
  * If someone is actively looking, there are usually deeper issues
  * Counteroffers rarely work long-term
* **Root causes**: Understand why they're leaving
  * Lack of growth opportunities?
  * Poor relationship with manager?
  * Misalignment with company direction?
  * Better opportunities elsewhere?
* **Retention strategies**:
  * Regular career conversations and growth planning
  * Competitive compensation and equity refreshes
  * Recognition and visibility for contributions
  * Challenging and meaningful work

**Non-Regrettable Attrition:**

These are people who are not meeting expectations or are poor culture fits. Sometimes it's best for everyone if they move on.

* Best solution for all (minimal blast radius)
* Make sure it is amicable and well communicated to team members
* Be in control of the narrative
* Provide fair severance and references where appropriate
* Conduct exit interviews to learn from the experience

### Attrition Benchmarks

* Startup attrition and tenure at startups is typically in the 2-3 years range
* Startups that are doing well and growing see tenure in the 5-7 year range
* Industry benchmarks vary by sector (10-15% annual attrition is typical for tech)

{: .note }
> **Startup years are like dog-years**: 1 year at a startup can feel like 7 years at a larger company since things move way faster and at an accelerated pace.

### Managing Departures

When someone resigns:

1. **Have a candid conversation**: Understand their reasons (exit interview)
2. **Manage the transition**: Define knowledge transfer and handoff plan
3. **Communicate to the team**: Be transparent about the departure
4. **Conduct team retrospective**: What can we learn?
5. **Plan backfill**: Do we hire a replacement or redistribute work?

### Reducing Regrettable Attrition

* Conduct regular stay interviews (not just exit interviews)
* Monitor team engagement and satisfaction
* Provide clear growth paths and opportunities
* Recognize and reward top performers
* Address toxic team members quickly
* Foster strong manager-employee relationships
* Ensure competitive compensation

{: .important }
> The best retention strategy is hiring the right people, giving them meaningful work, and treating them well. Everything else is damage control.
