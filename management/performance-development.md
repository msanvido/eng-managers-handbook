## Overview

Managing performance and fostering development are core responsibilities of engineering management. This section covers the tools and processes for evaluating performance, setting goals, handling underperformance, and creating growth opportunities for your team.

## Performance Reviews

Performance reviews should be done regularly to provide structured feedback, align on expectations, and support career growth.

### Performance Review Cadence

* **Quarterly check-ins**: Informal performance discussions
* **Mid-year reviews**: Formal review at 6-month mark
* **Annual reviews**: Comprehensive performance evaluation
* **Continuous feedback**: Don't wait for reviews—give feedback in real-time

### Components of a Performance Review

**Self-Assessment:**
* Ask engineers to reflect on their accomplishments
* Identify areas where they exceeded expectations
* Highlight challenges and areas for improvement
* Set goals for the next period

**Manager Assessment:**
* Evaluate against defined expectations for the level
* Assess technical skills, collaboration, and impact
* Provide specific examples (both positive and constructive)
* Be honest but compassionate

**Peer Feedback:**
* Collect input from area leads and collaborators
* 360-degree feedback for senior engineers
* Anonymous surveys for broader team sentiment

**Engineering Org Ranking:**
* Stack ranking (if used) should be done thoughtfully
* Consider impact, not just output
* Account for different roles and responsibilities
* Be transparent about the process

### Performance Review Best Practices

* Prepare thoroughly—don't wing it
* Use specific examples and data
* Focus on growth and development, not just evaluation
* Align reviews with company values and engineering principles
* No surprises—issues should be discussed in real-time, not saved for reviews
* Document everything for future reference and compensation decisions

## OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

OKRs are a goal-setting framework to align company, team, and individual objectives.

### OKR Structure

**Objectives:** Qualitative goals that are ambitious and inspirational
* Example: "Build a world-class observability platform"

**Key Results:** Quantitative measures that define success
* Example: "Reduce mean time to detection (MTTD) to under 5 minutes"
* Example: "Achieve 99.99% uptime for critical services"
* Example: "Onboard 10 teams to the new monitoring stack"

### OKR Cadence

* **Quarterly OKRs**: Most common cycle (aligns with business planning)
* **Annual OKRs**: Set high-level company and org objectives
* **Weekly check-ins**: Track progress and adjust as needed
* **End-of-quarter review**: Grade OKRs and reflect on learnings

### Writing Good OKRs

**Good Objectives:**
* Ambitious but achievable
* Aligned with company strategy
* Clear and inspiring
* Time-bound (typically quarterly)

**Good Key Results:**
* Measurable and specific
* Outcome-focused, not activity-focused
* Challenging but realistic (70% achievement is typical)
* Limited in number (3-5 per objective)

### OKR Anti-Patterns

* Using OKRs as a task list
* Setting too many OKRs (overwhelms teams)
* Making them too easy (100% achievement means not ambitious enough)
* Treating OKRs as performance evaluations (they're separate)
* Not reviewing or updating them regularly

## Professional Development and Growth

Invest in your team's continuous learning and career progression.

### Growth Opportunities

* **Mentorship programs**: Pair junior and senior engineers
* **Stretch assignments**: Give engineers projects slightly beyond their current level
* **Technical leadership**: Opportunities to lead design reviews, RFC processes, etc.
* **Cross-functional projects**: Expose engineers to product, sales, customer success
* **Conference attendance**: Reward top performers with conference sponsorship
* **Learning budgets**: Allocate budget for courses, books, certifications
* **Internal tech talks**: Encourage knowledge sharing through lunch & learns

### Career Development Conversations

* Discuss career goals and aspirations regularly (separate from performance reviews)
* Create individual development plans (IDPs)
* Identify skill gaps and create plans to address them
* Provide visibility and exposure to senior leadership
* Champion your team members for promotions and opportunities

## Leveling and Promotions

{: .warning }
> This is a can of worms and a very hot topic. Here is my personal view and experience.

Leveling is related to performance review but puts visible (internal or external) structures around it.

### When to Introduce Leveling

I firmly believe that until the company passes existential threats (i.e. runway is not measured in months), leveling will be just a distraction and a cause of unhappiness. Wait as long as you can and focus the team on delivering the product/milestones.

**Introduce leveling when:**
* You need a formal way for junior engineers to grow inside the company
* You need to unify performance expectations across the org
* You have a benchmark for external hiring—so they know what level they're joining at

**Leveling is NOT:**
* A way to compensate people! Yes, levels are a floor for comp but they should be loosely coupled
* A guarantee of promotion—promotions are earned, not automatic with tenure

### Building a Leveling Framework

* **Don't build from scratch**: Leverage existing frameworks from industry leaders
  * [progression.fyi](https://progression.fyi)
  * [levels.fyi](https://levels.fyi)
  * Company engineering blogs (Dropbox, Rent the Runway, CircleCI, etc.)
* **Adapt, don't copy**: Customize for your company's values and needs
* **Define clear criteria**: Technical skills, scope, impact, leadership, collaboration
* **Make it transparent**: Engineers should understand what's expected at each level
* **Keep it simple**: Too many levels creates confusion and politics

### Promotion Process

* **Self-nomination**: Engineers should advocate for themselves
* **Manager sponsorship**: Manager makes the case for promotion
* **Calibration**: Cross-team review to ensure consistency
* **Promotion committee**: Senior leaders review and approve
* **Timeline**: Regular promotion cycles (e.g., twice per year)
* **Communication**: Announce promotions to celebrate achievements

### Promotion Criteria

Promotions should be based on:
* **Impact**: Has the engineer already been performing at the next level?
* **Consistency**: Sustained performance over time, not one big project
* **Scope**: Increased responsibility and influence
* **Leadership**: Technical leadership, mentorship, and culture contribution

{: .important }
> Engineers should already be performing at the next level before being promoted. Promotions recognize past performance, not future potential.

## Performance Bonuses and Recognition

* **Performance bonuses**: Annual or quarterly bonuses based on performance
* **Spot bonuses**: Immediate recognition for exceptional work
* **Gifts**: Small tokens of appreciation for going above and beyond

**Budget Planning:**
* Setup a quarterly/yearly budget org-wide for bonuses and recognition
* Prepare it ahead of time so you have it ready when needed
* Be fair and consistent across teams

## Reassignments

Sometimes people's skills or personal circumstances are better addressed by moving them to a different team.

* Use reassignments to address skill mismatches or team dynamics issues
* Be thoughtful—reassignments can be perceived as punitive
* Communicate clearly and involve all parties (employee, current manager, new manager)
* Give the engineer a chance to succeed in the new environment

## Performance Improvement Plans (PIPs)

A PIP is a last resort to get somebody back on track. This is a formal process to address performance issues.

### When to Use a PIP

* Performance issues have been discussed in 1:1s and are not improving
* You have clear documentation of performance problems
* You've provided support, resources, and feedback
* The employee understands they are not meeting expectations

### PIP Principles

* **You must be OK with ANY outcome**: PIPs can result in improvement or separation
* **It will impact your relationship**: The employee-manager relationship will change
* **Set clear expectations and outcomes**: Specific, measurable goals
* **Work with HR/leadership**: Ensure process is followed correctly
* **Define timeline**: Typically 30, 60, or 90 days
* **Provide support**: Resources, coaching, clear feedback

### PIP Success Rates

* In my experience: ~20% success rate (employee improves and stays)
* ~80% result in separation (either termination or resignation)
* The 20% who succeed often become very loyal and improved employees

### Philosophy on PIPs

Some companies don't believe in PIPs and prefer to move directly to separation. Personally, I think PIPs are important not only for the employee but as a signal that the company cares about making people successful and did everything possible before separation.

## Separation

This is the last resort! There is no way back, and the blast radius's morale impact must be considered.

### When Separation is Necessary

* Performance issues not resolved through PIP
* Violation of code of conduct or company policies
* Role elimination (layoffs)
* Mutual agreement that it's not a fit

### Managing Separations

**Before the Conversation:**
* Work with HR and legal to ensure compliance
* Prepare severance package (if applicable)
* Plan communication to the team
* Have a transition plan for the employee's work

**During the Conversation:**
* **Do not delegate to HR**: As the manager, you should deliver the news
* Be direct and compassionate
* Explain the decision clearly
* Listen to their response
* Discuss logistics (last day, benefits, references)

**After the Separation:**
* Communicate to the team promptly
* Hold 1:1s with team members to address concerns
* Redistribute work or start hiring process
* Conduct a retrospective: What could we have done differently?

### Key Principles

* **No surprises**: Employees should know they are not performing at the expected level
* **Treat people with dignity**: Even in separation, show respect
* **Be in control of the narrative**: Communicate proactively to the team
* **Learn from it**: What could you have done differently in hiring, onboarding, or management?

{: .warning }
> Separation decisions are hard but sometimes necessary. Handle them with care, compassion, and professionalism. How you handle departures defines your culture.
