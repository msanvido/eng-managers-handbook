## Overview

Managing resources—budget, headcount, tools, and infrastructure—is a critical responsibility for engineering leaders. This section covers how to plan, allocate, and monitor resources effectively to enable your team's success while maintaining fiscal responsibility.

## Budget Planning and Allocation

### Annual Budget Planning

Engineering budgets typically follow the company's fiscal year and include several major categories:

**1. Personnel Costs (typically 70-80% of budget)**
- Base salaries
- Bonuses and commissions
- Equity compensation
- Benefits (health insurance, 401k matching, etc.)
- Payroll taxes
- Recruiting costs (recruiters, job boards, interview expenses)

**2. Infrastructure and Tools (typically 10-20% of budget)**
- Cloud infrastructure (AWS, GCP, Azure)
- SaaS tools and licenses
- Development tools and IDEs
- CI/CD platforms
- Monitoring and observability tools

**3. Professional Development (typically 2-5% of budget)**
- Conference attendance
- Training and courses
- Certifications
- Books and learning resources

**4. Other Operating Expenses**
- Office equipment (laptops, monitors, etc.)
- Travel and accommodation
- Team events and offsites
- Vendor services and consultants

### Budget Planning Process

**Step 1: Review Historical Spending**
- Analyze previous year's actual spending vs budget
- Identify trends and anomalies
- Understand what drove overages or underspending

**Step 2: Forecast Headcount**
- Work with leadership to understand growth plans
- Account for attrition (typically 10-15% annually in tech)
- Plan for backfills and new roles
- Consider hiring timeline (allow 2-3 months from req to start date)

**Step 3: Calculate Personnel Costs**
- Current team salaries and benefits
- Planned raises and promotions (typically 3-5% merit increases)
- New hires (use market rates for your location/level)
- Bonus pool (if applicable, typically 10-20% of base)
- Recruiting costs (estimate $15-30k per hire)

**Step 4: Estimate Infrastructure and Tools**
- Review current infrastructure spending trends
- Account for growth in usage (usually scales with team size)
- Plan for new tools or platform migrations
- Factor in price increases (cloud costs typically grow 10-20% YoY)

**Step 5: Allocate Professional Development**
- Set per-person budget (typically $1,500-3,000/person/year)
- Reserve budget for high-value conferences
- Plan for team-wide training initiatives

**Step 6: Build in Contingency**
- Add 5-10% buffer for unexpected costs
- Plan for emergency hiring needs
- Account for market adjustments

### Budget Allocation Strategy

**Top-Down Approach:**
- Finance/leadership sets overall engineering budget
- VP Eng/CTO allocates across teams/departments
- Team leads manage their allocated budgets

**Bottom-Up Approach:**
- Teams propose budgets based on their needs
- Leadership reviews and adjusts
- More accurate but time-consuming

**Recommended Hybrid Approach:**
- Leadership provides budget guidance and constraints
- Teams submit proposals within those parameters
- Collaborative review and final allocation

### Quarterly Budget Reviews

Monitor and adjust budgets quarterly:

**What to Review:**
- Actual vs planned spending (overall and by category)
- Headcount vs plan (hires, departures, open reqs)
- Cloud and infrastructure costs vs forecast
- Tool utilization and ROI

**Common Issues:**
- **Underspending on headcount**: Hiring taking longer than planned (common)
- **Infrastructure overspending**: Underestimated growth or inefficiencies
- **Tool sprawl**: Redundant or underutilized tools

**Adjustments:**
- Reallocate from underspent categories
- Request additional budget if justified
- Cut or reduce underutilized resources

## OpEx vs CapEx Management

Understanding the difference between Operating Expenses (OpEx) and Capital Expenses (CapEx) is important for financial planning and reporting.

### Operating Expenses (OpEx)

**Definition**: Day-to-day expenses required to run the business, deducted from revenue in the same fiscal period.

**Engineering OpEx Examples:**
- Employee salaries and benefits
- Cloud infrastructure costs (AWS, GCP, Azure)
- SaaS subscriptions (Jira, Slack, GitHub, etc.)
- Consulting and contractor fees
- Office supplies and minor equipment
- Training and professional development

**Characteristics:**
- Fully tax-deductible in the year incurred
- Predictable and recurring
- Easier to scale up or down
- Shows up on P&L (Profit & Loss statement)

### Capital Expenses (CapEx)

**Definition**: Investments in assets that provide value over multiple years, depreciated over time.

**Engineering CapEx Examples:**
- On-premise data center equipment
- Large software license purchases (multi-year)
- Major infrastructure investments
- Proprietary technology development (in some cases)

**Characteristics:**
- Depreciated over useful life (typically 3-5 years)
- Shows up on balance sheet
- Requires larger upfront investment
- Less flexible—harder to cancel or reduce

### The Shift to OpEx (Cloud Era)

**Traditional (CapEx-Heavy):**
- Buy servers and data center space
- Large upfront capital investment
- Fixed capacity, often over-provisioned
- Depreciate hardware over 3-5 years

**Modern (OpEx-Heavy):**
- Pay for cloud infrastructure as you use it
- No upfront investment
- Scale up/down based on demand
- Predictable monthly costs

**Why Most Startups Prefer OpEx:**
1. **Cash preservation**: No large upfront investments
2. **Flexibility**: Scale resources with business needs
3. **Tax benefits**: Immediate deduction vs depreciation
4. **Reduced risk**: Avoid obsolescence of owned equipment
5. **Predictability**: Easier to forecast and budget

### Managing OpEx Efficiently

**Monitor Cloud Spending:**
- Set up cost alerts and budgets
- Review monthly spending by service and team
- Identify waste (unused resources, over-provisioned instances)
- Implement auto-scaling and rightsizing
- Use reserved instances or savings plans for predictable workloads

**Optimize SaaS Tools:**
- Audit licenses quarterly (remove unused seats)
- Consolidate overlapping tools
- Negotiate enterprise agreements for volume discounts
- Consider annual vs monthly billing (typically 15-20% savings)

**Track and Report:**
- Monthly OpEx vs budget reports
- Cost per engineer metrics
- Cost per customer/transaction (unit economics)
- Trending and forecasts

## Professional Development Budget

Investing in your team's growth is essential for retention and capability building.

### Setting Professional Development Budgets

**Industry Benchmarks:**
- Startups: $1,500-2,500 per person per year
- Mid-size companies: $2,500-4,000 per person per year
- Large tech companies: $4,000-6,000+ per person per year

**What to Include:**
- Conference attendance (ticket, travel, accommodation)
- Online courses and certifications
- Books and learning materials
- Workshops and training programs
- Professional membership fees

### Allocation Strategies

**Per-Person Budget:**
- Each engineer gets allocated budget (e.g., $2,000/year)
- Self-directed spending within guidelines
- Use-it-or-lose-it (resets annually)
- Encourages ownership of development

**Team Pool:**
- Budget allocated to team, not individuals
- Manager approves requests
- Allows flexibility for high-value opportunities
- Can create equity issues if not managed carefully

**Hybrid Approach (Recommended):**
- Base allocation per person ($1,500)
- Additional team pool for high-value opportunities
- Manager discretion for exceptional performers

### Guidelines and Policies

**Approval Process:**
- Small expenses (<$500): Manager approval
- Large expenses (>$500): Requires written proposal
- Conferences: Consider business value and fairness

**Reimbursement:**
- Clear policy on what's covered
- Expense report requirements
- Timelines for reimbursement

**ROI Expectations:**
- Share learnings with team (brown bag, doc, etc.)
- Apply new skills to work
- For expensive programs, may require tenure commitment

### Popular Learning Resources

**Online Learning:**
- Pluralsight, Udemy, Coursera, O'Reilly Learning
- Frontend Masters, Egghead.io
- AWS Training and Certification

**Conferences Worth Attending:**
- QCon, Velocity, KubeCon
- AWS re:Invent, Google I/O
- Domain-specific conferences (React Conf, PyCon, etc.)
- LeadDev for engineering managers

## Compensation Management

Compensation is often your largest budget category and critical for attracting and retaining talent.

### Compensation Philosophy

**Key Principles:**

1. **Competitive**: Pay at or above market rates for your location and stage
2. **Equitable**: Similar roles and performance = similar pay
3. **Transparent**: Clear bands and criteria (even if numbers aren't shared)
4. **Performance-based**: Top performers earn meaningfully more
5. **Market-informed**: Regularly benchmark against industry data

### Compensation Components

**Base Salary:**
- Fixed annual salary
- Primary component (60-80% of total comp for ICs)
- Based on role, level, location, and market rates

**Variable Compensation:**
- Performance bonuses (0-20% of base)
- Commissions (for sales-adjacent roles)
- Spot bonuses for exceptional work

**Equity:**
- Stock options (common at startups)
- RSUs (more common at later-stage/public companies)
- Typically 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff
- Significant component (20-40% of total comp)

**Benefits:**
- Health insurance
- 401k matching
- PTO and holidays
- Parental leave
- Other perks (meals, transit, etc.)

### Setting Compensation Bands

**Step 1: Define Levels**
- Use industry-standard leveling framework
- Clear criteria for each level
- (See [Performance & Development](./performance-development.html#leveling-and-promotions))

**Step 2: Benchmark Market Rates**
- Use compensation data sources:
  - [levels.fyi](https://levels.fyi)
  - [Pave](https://www.pave.com)
  - [Radford Survey](https://radford.aon.com)
  - [Carta Compensation Data](https://carta.com/compensation/)
- Account for location (SF/NYC vs remote vs international)
- Consider company stage (early-stage pays more equity, less cash)

**Step 3: Create Bands**
- Set min, midpoint, and max for each level
- Typical spread: 20-30% from min to max
- Example for Senior Engineer:
  - Min: $140k
  - Midpoint: $160k
  - Max: $180k

**Step 4: Position Current Employees**
- Map current salaries to bands
- Identify outliers (above max, below min)
- Plan adjustments over time

### Compensation Review Process

**Cadence:**
- Annual comprehensive reviews
- Mid-year adjustments for promotions or market corrections
- Ad-hoc for retention situations

**Annual Review Process:**

**1. Prepare (2-3 months before review date):**
- Review budget allocation for raises
- Gather performance data
- Benchmark current comp vs market
- Identify high performers and retention risks

**2. Manager Proposals:**
- Managers propose raises for their team
- Justify with performance data
- Highlight retention risks
- Stay within allocated budget

**3. Calibration:**
- Cross-team review of proposals
- Ensure consistency and equity
- Senior leadership approval
- Final budget allocation

**4. Communication:**
- Managers deliver news in 1:1s
- Provide context and rationale
- Document in writing
- Address questions and concerns

### Types of Compensation Adjustments

**Merit Increases (Annual):**
- Based on performance
- Typical ranges:
  - Meets expectations: 3-5%
  - Exceeds expectations: 5-8%
  - Far exceeds: 8-12%
- Applied across the board annually

**Promotions:**
- Role level change
- Typically 10-20% increase
- Should bring person to new level's band midpoint
- Include equity refresh

**Market Adjustments:**
- Correct for market movement
- Not performance-based
- Bring comp in line with current market
- May be done off-cycle

**Retention Adjustments:**
- Reactive to counter-offers or flight risk
- Higher risk—signals you need an offer to get raise
- Better: proactive market adjustments for key people

**Equity Refreshes:**
- Annual grants to top performers
- Keep equity compensation meaningful over time
- Typically smaller than initial grant
- Vest over 4 years (same as initial grant)

### Compression and Inversion Issues

**Compression**: Senior people's salaries don't reflect seniority
- Caused by: Market rate increases, not adjusting existing salaries
- Fix: Regular market adjustments for entire team

**Inversion**: New hires paid more than existing employees in same role
- Caused by: Hot market, not adjusting existing salaries
- Fix: Market adjustments when hiring at higher rates

### Compensation Conversations

**Tips for Discussing Comp:**
- Be prepared with data and rationale
- Listen to concerns and feedback
- Don't make promises you can't keep
- If news is bad, acknowledge disappointment
- Focus on total comp (base + equity + benefits)
- Discuss development areas for future increases

**Handling Compensation Requests:**
- Take time to review the request
- Gather market data and budget
- Consider total compensation, not just base
- If yes: process through normal review cycle
- If no: explain rationale and path forward
- If maybe: outline what would change decision
